About Me

I am a PhD candidate in political economy at the Woodrow Wilson School of Public and International Affairs at Princeton University expecting to defend my dissertation this September. I have academic training in microeconomic theory, statistics, political economy and a number of health related fields including health economics, epidemiology, and demography.

I enjoy using quantitative methods to answer applied questions in a number of areas such as business, public policy, politics, sports, and health. In my current research, I study public policy issues---especially in healthcare---from economic and political perspectives. Some of the questions that I am currently thinking about include:

  • How should we design health insurance so that individuals get necessary care and are sufficiently protected from financial risk, but do not spend too inefficiently due to moral hazard?
  • How can data science and statistical modeling be used to improve health care delivery and/or the cost-effectiveness of health care?
  • Why are there differences in health outcomes across and within OECD countries and can understanding these differences inform public policy?

My interest in health stems partly from my enjoyment of sports and the outdoors. In my pre-PhD life, I was a rather unsuccessful collegiate baseball player at UC San Diego whose career was cut short by a shoulder injury. I still enjoy trying to relive my days as an athlete by playing sports when my shoulder permits (usually basketball). I also somehow enjoy lifting up heavy objects and then putting them back down.
